Summarized Notes

Condensed, high-yield summaries for each CFA Level I topic. Use these for review and reinforcement — then test yourself with the quizzes.

1 · Ethical & Professional Standards

Exam weight: 15–20% (the largest topic)

The Code of Ethics (6 components)

  • Act with integrity, competence, diligence, and respect.
  • Place client and profession interests above your own.
  • Use reasonable care and independent professional judgment.
  • Practice and encourage others to practice ethically.
  • Promote the integrity of capital markets.
  • Maintain and improve professional competence.

The 7 Standards of Professional Conduct

  • I. Professionalism — Knowledge of the law; Independence & objectivity; Misrepresentation; Misconduct.
  • II. Integrity of Capital Markets — Material nonpublic information (insider trading); Market manipulation.
  • III. Duties to Clients — Loyalty/prudence/care; Fair dealing; Suitability; Performance presentation; Confidentiality.
  • IV. Duties to Employers — Loyalty; Additional compensation arrangements; Responsibilities of supervisors.
  • V. Investment Analysis — Diligence & reasonable basis; Communication with clients; Record retention.
  • VI. Conflicts of Interest — Disclosure of conflicts; Priority of transactions; Referral fees.
  • VII. Responsibilities as a Member/Candidate — Conduct in the CFA Program; Reference to CFA Institute & designation.
Exam tip: When the law and the Code conflict, follow the stricter of the two. Always choose disclosure, then follow employer policy.

GIPS (Global Investment Performance Standards)

  • Voluntary, ethical standards for calculating and presenting investment performance.
  • Apply to firms, not individuals; compliance is all-or-nothing (firm-wide).
  • Goal: fair representation and full disclosure; comparability across firms.

2 · Quantitative Methods

Time Value of Money

  • Money has a time value: a dollar today > a dollar tomorrow.
  • Master PV/FV of single sums, ordinary annuities, annuities due, and perpetuities.
  • Compounding frequency matters — convert stated rates to effective rates.

Rates & Returns

  • Holding period return, arithmetic vs. geometric mean (geometric ≤ arithmetic), money-weighted (IRR) vs. time-weighted returns.
  • Nominal risk-free + risk premiums (inflation, default, liquidity, maturity).

Statistics & Probability

  • Measures of central tendency & dispersion (variance, standard deviation, CV = σ/mean).
  • Skewness (right/positive = mean>median) and kurtosis (leptokurtic = fat tails).
  • Probability rules, expected value, covariance & correlation, Bayes' formula.

Distributions & Sampling

  • Normal distribution: ~68/95/99% within 1/2/3 σ. Standard normal Z.
  • Central Limit Theorem: sampling distribution of the mean → normal as n grows.
  • Confidence intervals; t-distribution for small samples / unknown variance.

Hypothesis Testing & Regression

  • Null vs. alternative; Type I (reject true null, = significance α) vs. Type II error.
  • p-value < α → reject the null. One- vs. two-tailed tests.
  • Simple linear regression: slope, intercept, R², and the standard error of estimate.
High-yield: Know when to use which mean (geometric for compounding/multi-period returns), and the difference between money-weighted and time-weighted returns.

3 · Economics

Microeconomics

  • Demand & supply, elasticity (price, income, cross), consumer/producer surplus.
  • Costs of production: economies/diseconomies of scale; breakeven & shutdown points.
  • Market structures: perfect competition, monopolistic competition, oligopoly, monopoly — pricing power rises across this spectrum.

Macroeconomics

  • GDP (expenditure & income approaches), aggregate demand/supply, business cycle phases.
  • Inflation types (demand-pull, cost-push); CPI; unemployment measures.
  • Monetary policy (central banks, interest rates, money supply) vs. fiscal policy (govt spending & taxes).

International Trade & Currencies

  • Comparative vs. absolute advantage; trade restrictions (tariffs, quotas).
  • Exchange rate quotes (direct/indirect), spot vs. forward, currency appreciation/depreciation.
  • Balance of payments: current account + capital/financial account.
Exam tip: Be fluent reading exchange-rate quotes. A rise in the base/price currency means the price currency is depreciating relative to the base.

4 · Financial Statement Analysis

The Core Statements

  • Income statement — revenues − expenses = net income; understand revenue recognition.
  • Balance sheet — Assets = Liabilities + Equity (the accounting equation).
  • Cash flow statement — Operating (CFO), Investing (CFI), Financing (CFF); direct vs. indirect method.

Reporting Standards

  • IFRS vs. US GAAP differences (e.g., inventory: LIFO permitted under GAAP, not IFRS).
  • Accrual accounting, the matching principle, and quality-of-earnings flags.

Analysis Areas

  • Ratios — liquidity, solvency, activity, profitability, and DuPont decomposition of ROE.
  • Inventories — FIFO vs. LIFO vs. weighted average; effects on COGS, income, and taxes.
  • Long-lived assets — capitalize vs. expense; depreciation methods; impairment.
  • Income taxes — deferred tax assets/liabilities; permanent vs. temporary differences.
  • Non-current liabilities — bonds payable, leases.
High-yield: In rising prices, FIFO → higher net income & inventory; LIFO → higher COGS, lower taxes, lower inventory. DuPont: ROE = Net Margin × Asset Turnover × Financial Leverage.

5 · Corporate Issuers

Governance & Stakeholders

  • Corporate governance: aligning management with shareholders; board structure.
  • Stakeholder theory; principal–agent conflicts; ESG considerations.

Capital Budgeting

  • Decision rules: NPV (accept if > 0) and IRR (accept if > required return).
  • NPV is theoretically superior; watch for conflicts with IRR on mutually exclusive projects.
  • Use incremental after-tax cash flows; ignore sunk costs; include opportunity costs.

Cost of Capital

  • WACC = weighted cost of debt (after-tax) + preferred + equity.
  • Cost of equity via CAPM or dividend discount model.

Leverage & Working Capital

  • Operating leverage (fixed operating costs) and financial leverage (fixed financing costs); combined = DTL.
  • Working capital management: cash conversion cycle, short-term funding.
Exam tip: Use the after-tax cost of debt in WACC: rd(1 − tax rate). Higher leverage magnifies both gains and losses.

6 · Equity Investments

Markets & Indexes

  • Market organization: dealers, brokers, exchanges; long/short positions; margin trading.
  • Index construction: price-weighted, value (market-cap) weighted, equal-weighted — know rebalancing effects.

Market Efficiency

  • Efficient Market Hypothesis: weak, semi-strong, strong forms.
  • Behavioral biases and documented anomalies.

Equity Valuation

  • Dividend Discount Model and the Gordon (constant) growth model.
  • Multiplier/relative valuation: P/E, P/B, P/S, EV/EBITDA.
  • Free-cash-flow and asset-based approaches (overview).
High-yield: Gordon growth: V₀ = D₁ / (r − g). Only valid when r > g and g is constant. Price-weighted indexes are distorted by high-priced stocks and stock splits.

7 · Fixed Income

Bond Basics

  • Features: issuer, maturity, coupon, par; bond indenture and covenants.
  • Embedded options: callable, putable, convertible.

Pricing & Yields

  • Price = PV of coupons + PV of par. Price and yield move inversely.
  • Premium (coupon > yield), par (coupon = yield), discount (coupon < yield).
  • Yield measures: YTM, current yield, spot/forward rates, the yield curve.

Risk & Sensitivity

  • Duration — sensitivity to interest-rate changes (Macaulay, modified, effective).
  • Convexity — the curvature correction; improves the duration estimate for large moves.
  • Credit risk: default, spread, and downgrade risk; credit ratings.
High-yield: %ΔPrice ≈ −ModDuration × Δy + ½ × Convexity × (Δy)². Longer maturity, lower coupon, and lower yield all increase duration.

8 · Derivatives

Instrument Types

  • Forwards — customized OTC; Futures — standardized, exchange-traded, marked to market daily.
  • Swaps — exchange of cash-flow streams (e.g., fixed-for-floating).
  • Options — calls (right to buy) and puts (right to sell); long has the right, short has the obligation.

Pricing & Arbitrage

  • No-arbitrage pricing; the cost-of-carry model for forwards.
  • Put–call parity: C + PV(X) = P + S₀.
  • Option value = intrinsic value + time value.
Exam tip: Futures vs. forwards — daily settlement (mark-to-market) and clearinghouse credit guarantee are the key distinctions. Memorize put–call parity cold.

9 · Alternative Investments

Categories

  • Real estate — direct & REITs; income + appreciation.
  • Private equity — venture capital, buyouts; J-curve return pattern.
  • Hedge funds — varied strategies; often "2 and 20" fee structure.
  • Commodities — exposure via futures; contango vs. backwardation.
  • Infrastructure & private debt.

Characteristics

  • Low correlation with traditional assets → diversification benefit.
  • Illiquidity, higher fees, less transparency, and complex valuation.
  • Fee structure: management fee + incentive/performance fee (watch hurdle rates & high-water marks).
High-yield: "2 and 20" = 2% management fee + 20% of profits. A high-water mark prevents charging incentive fees twice on the same gains.

10 · Portfolio Management

The Portfolio Process

  • Planning → Execution → Feedback. The Investment Policy Statement (IPS) drives everything.
  • IPS captures objectives (return, risk) and constraints (liquidity, time horizon, taxes, legal, unique).

Risk & Return

  • Portfolio return = weighted average; portfolio risk depends on correlations — diversification reduces risk.
  • Efficient frontier; the optimal risky portfolio + risk-free asset → Capital Market Line (CML).

CAPM & the SML

  • Systematic (market) risk cannot be diversified away; unsystematic risk can.
  • Beta measures systematic risk. The Security Market Line plots required return vs. beta.
  • CAPM: E(R) = Rf + β[E(Rm) − Rf].
High-yield: Only systematic risk is rewarded with return. Above the SML = undervalued; below = overvalued. Know the order of the portfolio management process.
